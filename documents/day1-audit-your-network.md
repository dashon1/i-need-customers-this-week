# Day 1: Audit Your Network

## Find the Customers Who Are Already in Your Contacts

Before you run any ads, build any content, or try any clever marketing strategy, do this first: look at the people you already know.

Most solopreneurs and small business owners are sitting on a goldmine of potential customers — people who already know them, like them, and would buy from them if they just asked. They just haven't asked.

Today is the day you map that goldmine.

---

## Why Your Next Customer Is Probably Already in Your Contacts

Think about the last 5 times you bought something. How many of those purchases were from a stranger versus someone you already knew or had been referred to?

The research consistently shows that people buy from people they know. Warm relationships convert at 5–10x the rate of cold outreach. A former colleague who knows your work is infinitely more likely to hire you than someone who found you on Google.

Most people, when they say "I need customers," immediately think about how to find new people. But the fastest path to revenue is almost always through people who already exist in your world — they just need to know you're available and ready to help.

---

## Exercise 1: The 3-Category Contact Sort

Open your phone contacts, LinkedIn connections, or email inbox. Sort the people you know into these three categories:

**Category A: People who could hire you or buy from you directly**
These are people in your target market — business owners if you do B2B, or individuals who match your customer profile if you do B2C. They have the problem you solve and the means to pay for the solution.

Write down every name you can think of in this category. Don't filter yet. Just list.

**Category B: People who know someone who could hire you or buy from you**
These are connectors — people with wide networks, influence in your industry, or relationships with many of your ideal customers. A well-connected accountant knows dozens of small business owners. A popular local blogger knows hundreds of your potential customers.

**Category C: Former colleagues, classmates, and dormant relationships**
People you used to know well but haven't spoken to in a while. They know your work. They know your character. If they need what you do, or know someone who does, they're likely to recommend you — they just haven't thought about it because you haven't reminded them you exist.

---

## Exercise 2: The 10 Names List

From your Category A list, pick the 10 people who are:
- Most likely to need what you do right now
- Most likely to respond to a personal message from you
- Able to pay for it

Write their names on a piece of paper or in a note on your phone. These 10 people are your Day 1 outreach targets.

---

## Exercise 3: The "Who Do They Know?" Map

For your top 10 Category A contacts, and your Category B connectors, answer this question: "Who do they know that I should meet?"

This is not about being transactional — it's about recognizing that your network extends far beyond the people in your direct contacts. Each person in your network has a network. A warm introduction is 10x more valuable than a cold email.

---

## The Day 1 Outreach Script

For each of your 10 Category A names, send a short, personal message today. Not a pitch. Not a newsletter. A human message.

**Template (email):**
> Subject: Quick note from [Your Name]
>
> Hey [Name],
>
> I've been thinking about you lately — hope things are going well.
>
> I'm currently working with [TYPE OF CLIENT] on [PROBLEM YOU SOLVE] and it's been going really well. I've had a few spots open up this [week/month] and wanted to reach out to you first before I open them to new inquiries.
>
> Would you be open to a quick 15-minute call to see if there's a fit? No pressure either way.
>
> [Your Name]

**Template (text/DM):**
> Hey [Name], hope things are good! I'm taking on a few new [CLIENTS/PROJECTS] this week and thought of you. Would love to catch up if you have 15 minutes. Let me know!

**Key principles:**
- Keep it short. Long messages get skimmed or ignored.
- Make it personal. Use their name. Reference something real.
- Don't pitch in the first message. Just open a conversation.
- Ask a yes/no question at the end. "Would you be open to..." gets answered. "Let me know if you're interested" gets ignored.

- [ ] Identified 10 Category A names
